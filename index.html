What is this course about?

CSC343 is an introduction to database management systems. We'll explore how data is modelled, stored, queried, and kept consistent — and why those problems are more challenging than they look.

🗂Data modelling — how to represent real-world entities and their relationships using ERDs and the relational model
🔍Querying — relational algebra as the mathematical foundation, and SQL as the practical language you'll use in the real world
🏗Database design — functional dependencies, normal forms, and how to avoid the anomalies that come from poor schema design
⚙️System internals — indexes, query optimisation, and how the database engine actually executes your queries
🔐Transactions & concurrency — ACID properties, isolation levels, locking, and what happens when two users touch the same data at once

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  • Design entity-relationship diagrams (ERDs) that accurately model complex, real-world domains
  • Translate ERDs to relational schemas and implement them in PostgreSQL
  • Write correct and efficient SQL queries, including aggregation, subqueries, joins, and NULL handling
  • Express queries in relational algebra (RA) and understand the connection between RA and SQL
  • Identify functional dependencies and normalise a schema to BCNF or 3NF
  • Explain how indexes and query optimisation work, and make informed design decisions
  • Reason about transactions, isolation levels, and concurrency using 2PL and MVCC
  • Situate relational databases within the broader landscape of modern data systems (NoSQL, vector DBs, distributed systems)

Course Philosophy: Students First

I believe the best learning happens when students feel supported, not stressed. A few principles that shape how this course is run:

Better later is still better

The 5% between the midterm and final always shifts to whichever score is higher. If you struggled on the midterm but put in the work and did better on the final, that growth is reflected in your grade.

Good work takes iteration

Every Checkpoint has a resubmission window. Use the feedback, fix your work, and resubmit. Students who revise learn more than students who move on.

Each piece builds the next

Every assignment uses the same Research Network. The context builds over time and my goal is to make sure everything connects.

Low-stakes entry

Checkpoint 1 is intentionally small and forgiving. The goal is to orient you.

Partial credit beats zero

If you're at risk of missing a deadline, submit whatever you have. Partial credit is always better than a zero, and the resubmission window exists for exactly this situation.

Please reach out

If something is going wrong — personally, academically, or otherwise — please talk to me. I can't always fix things but I promise to always help you find a path forward.

Policies

Academic Integrity
All Checkpoints are individual work. Do not share or view another student's code. Discussions must stay conceptual — no solutions, no walkthroughs of your approach.
Plagiarism detection (MOSS, JPLAG, TurnItIn) runs on every submission.
AI tools (QuickTA, VoiceEx) are permitted as learning aids only. All submitted work must be entirely your own. Submitting AI-generated content without citation = plagiarism. Hallucinated sources = fabrication. Both are academic offences.
Tests and the final exam do not permit AI. Be prepared to write SQL, Python, and Java without assistance.
Academic integrity investigations persist even if you drop the course. If found guilty you may be re-enrolled and receive a failing grade.
Missed Work & Accommodations
Term workEmail the Course Coordinator with supporting documentation at least 1 week in advance, or within 1 week of an unexpected event. Declare illness on ACORN. Not automatic.
Missed midtermNo accommodations. In a serious documented emergency, contact the Course Coordinator immediately.
Missed final examFile an online petition within 72 hours. Late petitions are not considered.
Religious holidaysNotify the instructor at least 2 weeks before the relevant due date.
AccessibilityArrange accommodations through Accessibility Services before writing any assessment. Retroactive petitions are not accepted.
Resubmissions & Regrades
Each Checkpoint allows one resubmission (Checkpoint 1: within 48 hrs; Checkpoints 2–5: within 1 week of grade release). Resubmitted work is graded out of a maximum of 80%; your recorded grade is the higher of the two attempts.
Teaching staff may conduct oral interviews after any due date to verify understanding. Interview grades are final and cannot be appealed.
Re-mark requests must be submitted on MarkUs within 5 calendar days of receiving a grade. Re-marking may cover the full submission and may raise or lower your mark.
PCRS Homework
Must be completed before your registered lecture section. No late submissions accepted and no accommodations — completion is required to actively participate in lecture.
Full marks require: all exercises correct, all videos watched (or readings done), all reflections and follow-up components, any external surveys, and any linked platform activities.
